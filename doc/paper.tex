\begin{abstract}
Predicting hemodynamic parameters within thoracic aortic aneurysms (TAAs) remains a significant challenge in cardiovascular surgery, particularly when assessing rupture and dissection risk to guide surgical planning. Whilst traditional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations provide detailed flow information, they demand substantial computational resources and time-consuming mesh generation for patient-specific geometries. We present a physics-informed neural network (PINN) framework that addresses these limitations by learning from sparse CFD wall data---using approximately one third of available measurements---whilst simultaneously enforcing Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations with a learnable turbulent viscosity field. Our approach integrates a non-Newtonian Carreau-Yasuda viscosity model to capture the shear-thinning behaviour of blood, implements pulsatile inlet conditions derived from physiological waveforms, and predicts wall shear stress (WSS) patterns across six anatomically distinct aneurysm configurations. A gradient-norm adaptive loss weighting scheme dynamically balances data-driven and physics-based loss terms throughout training, while an alternating dual-optimiser strategy ensures the turbulent viscosity network receives dedicated gradient signal from the governing equations. Through this combination of techniques, the network achieves coefficient of determination values exceeding 0.85 for velocity components and 0.80 for pressure predictions, despite the significant data reduction. The trained model provides near-instantaneous inference, successfully reproducing complex haemodynamic features including recirculation zones and WSS distributions crucial for clinical decision-making.

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Physics-Informed Neural Networks, Thoracic Aortic Aneurysms, Haemodynamics, Wall Shear Stress, RANS, Adaptive Loss Weighting, Sparse Data Learning
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Thoracic aortic aneurysms (TAAs) are pathological dilations of the thoracic segment of the aorta, affecting approximately 6--10 per 100,000 persons annually \cite{ref1}. Left untreated, progressive aortic enlargement carries substantial risk of rupture or acute aortic dissection---events associated with mortality rates exceeding 50\% even with emergency surgical intervention \cite{ref2}. Clinicians must therefore balance the risk of watchful waiting against the morbidity of elective open surgical repair or endovascular aortic stenting, a decision that hinges on accurate assessment of the biomechanical forces acting on the aneurysm wall.

The haemodynamic environment within an aneurysm, characterised particularly by wall shear stress (WSS) patterns, has emerged as a critical determinant of vessel wall remodelling and rupture propensity \cite{ref3,ref4}. Regions subjected to abnormally low WSS tend to promote extracellular matrix degradation and continued aneurysm expansion, whilst areas experiencing elevated WSS may indicate flow impingement sites where the vessel wall experiences concentrated mechanical loading. Over the past two decades, computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has established itself as the principal tool for non-invasive haemodynamic analysis, offering detailed visualisation of three-dimensional flow structures and quantitative assessment of WSS distributions \cite{ref5,ref6}.

Despite its proven utility, CFD analysis confronts several persistent challenges that limit its clinical adoption. High-fidelity simulations demand considerable computational resources, particularly when modelling pulsatile blood flow through the cardiac cycle using Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) turbulence formulations. The process of generating suitable computational meshes for patient-specific geometries remains labour-intensive, requiring specialist expertise and often consuming hours of manual refinement to ensure mesh quality. Furthermore, CFD predictions exhibit sensitivity to numerous modelling assumptions---including inlet and outlet boundary conditions, turbulence closure schemes, and arterial wall compliance---which collectively introduce uncertainty into the haemodynamic assessments that clinicians rely upon for treatment planning.

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) \cite{raissi2019pinns} present an intriguing alternative paradigm that may address these limitations. Rather than treating governing equations and observational data as separate entities, PINNs embed physical laws directly into the learning process through carefully constructed loss functions that penalise deviations from known conservation principles. This synthesis of data-driven flexibility with physics-based rigour enables the network to interpolate sparse measurements whilst maintaining consistency with fundamental fluid mechanics.

\subsection{Contributions}

This investigation makes several distinct contributions to the application of physics-informed machine learning in vascular haemodynamics. First, we demonstrate that PINNs can achieve clinically relevant accuracy whilst learning from reduced datasets---specifically, training on approximately one third of available CFD wall measurements by enforcing conservation laws at collocation points sampled throughout the flow domain. Second, we implement a comprehensive treatment of physiologically realistic boundary conditions, including time-varying pulsatile inlet velocity profiles, zero-pressure outlet conditions, and rigorous enforcement of no-slip constraints at vessel walls.

Third, our formulation goes beyond the standard laminar Navier-Stokes equations by posing the Reynolds-averaged momentum and continuity equations with a \emph{learnable} turbulent viscosity field, output by a dedicated neural network branch. This enables the PINN to absorb the turbulence closure implicitly from the CFD training data, without requiring explicit transport equations for turbulence quantities. Fourth, we employ a gradient-norm adaptive loss weighting scheme \cite{ref_wang2021} that dynamically balances the competing loss terms throughout training, combined with an alternating dual-optimiser strategy that provides the turbulent viscosity network with a dedicated gradient signal from the physics residual. Our framework explicitly accounts for blood's non-Newtonian rheological behaviour through the Carreau-Yasuda viscosity model, recognising that shear-thinning effects become particularly pronounced in the low-velocity recirculation zones characteristic of aneurysm sacs. To assess generalisability, we conduct systematic evaluation across six distinct TAA configurations, varying both overall diameter (5~cm versus 6~cm) and morphological features (axisymmetric fusiform, asymmetric with downstream positioning, and asymmetric with upstream positioning), each at systolic and diastolic cardiac phases.

The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows. Section~2 positions our work within the broader context of CFD-based aneurysm analysis and emerging machine learning approaches. Section~3 details our methodology, including the problem formulation, network architecture, and physics-informed loss construction. Section~4 describes our experimental framework and the six aneurysm configurations under investigation. Section~5 presents quantitative performance metrics alongside qualitative visualisations of predicted flow fields. Section~6 discusses the implications of our findings for clinical application, addresses current limitations, and outlines promising directions for future development. Section~7 provides concluding remarks.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{CFD in Aneurysm Hemodynamics}

Computational fluid dynamics has been extensively applied to aortic aneurysm analysis. Bäumler et al.\ \cite{ref7} demonstrated the utility of patient-specific CFD for characterising aortic flow patterns validated against 4D flow MRI. Pasta et al.\ \cite{ref8} investigated the relationship between WSS distributions and aortic wall stress in thoracic aneurysms, establishing hemodynamic indices relevant to rupture risk stratification. However, CFD simulations typically require hours to days of computation per patient, limiting real-time clinical application.

\subsection{Machine Learning for Hemodynamics}

Traditional machine learning approaches have been applied to predict hemodynamic quantities from geometric features. Liang et al.\ \cite{ref9} used support vector machines to classify rupture risk based on morphological parameters of ascending aortic aneurysms. More recently, convolutional neural networks have been employed to predict CFD outcomes from 3D geometries \cite{ref10}, though these methods lack explicit physics encoding and require extensive training datasets.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Neural Networks}

Raissi et al.\ \cite{raissi2019pinns} introduced PINNs for solving forward and inverse problems in partial differential equations (PDEs). The key innovation is incorporating PDE residuals directly into the loss function, enabling networks to interpolate sparse data while satisfying conservation laws. PINNs have been successfully applied to fluid dynamics \cite{ref11}, heat transfer \cite{ref12}, and material modelling \cite{ref13}.

In cardiovascular applications, Arzani et al.\ \cite{ref14} applied PINNs to synthetic arterial flow problems, demonstrating superior performance over pure data-driven methods. Wang et al.\ \cite{ref_wang2021} identified gradient flow pathologies that arise when physics and data losses compete, and proposed gradient-norm balancing to adaptively reweight loss terms---a strategy we adopt and extend in this work. However, application of PINNs to complex patient-specific aortic aneurysm geometries with pulsatile flow, RANS turbulence modelling, and non-Newtonian rheology remains largely unexplored.

\section{Methodology}

We describe a physics-informed neural network (PINN) that approximates steady Reynolds-averaged haemodynamics for two cardiac phases (systolic and diastolic) in thoracic aortic aneurysm domains. The formulation couples data-driven fitting at the wall (pressure, WSS) with interior enforcement of the incompressible RANS equations, non-Newtonian Carreau-Yasuda viscosity, and a \emph{learnable} turbulent viscosity field. A gradient-norm adaptive weighting scheme dynamically balances the multiple loss terms, while an alternating dual-optimiser strategy ensures the turbulent viscosity network receives dedicated physics gradient signal. This section is structured as: problem formulation and governing equations (\S\ref{sec:problem}), non-dimensionalisation and standardisation (\S\ref{sec:nondim}), network architecture (\S\ref{sec:arch}), loss construction and weighting (\S\ref{sec:loss}), and training procedure (\S\ref{sec:training}).

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Problem Formulation and Governing Equations}
\label{sec:problem}
%==============================================================================

We consider incompressible flow in a bounded domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ representing the aneurysm lumen. The reference CFD was produced with the SST $k$--$\omega$ transition model and Carreau-Yasuda blood rheology; we therefore pose the \emph{Reynolds-averaged} momentum and continuity equations with an effective viscosity that combines molecular (shear-dependent) and turbulent contributions.

\subsubsection{RANS and Effective Viscosity}

The flow is governed by
\begin{equation}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 \quad \text{(continuity)}
\label{eq:continuity}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\rho \, \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{u} = -\nabla p + \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{eff}} \quad \text{(momentum, steady RANS)}
\label{eq:momentum}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{u} = (u,v,w)^T$ is the velocity, $p$ is pressure, $\rho = 1060$\,kg/m$^3$, and the deviatoric stress is
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{eff}} = \mu_{\text{eff}} \bigl( \nabla\mathbf{u} + (\nabla\mathbf{u})^T \bigr), \qquad \mu_{\text{eff}} = \mu(\dot{\gamma}) + \rho\,\nu_t.
\label{eq:tau_eff}
\end{equation}
Here $\mu(\dot{\gamma})$ is the shear-dependent molecular viscosity (Carreau--Yasuda) and $\nu_t$ is the turbulent kinematic viscosity, which we treat as a \emph{learnable field} $\nu_t(\mathbf{x}; t_{\text{phase}})$ output by a separate network (see \S\ref{sec:arch}). Rather than solving transport equations for turbulence quantities such as $k$ and $\omega$, the network learns a spatially-varying effective eddy viscosity directly from the CFD data through the physics residual. Temporal dependence is captured by training on two discrete cardiac phases---systole and diastole---encoded as a scalar $t_{\text{phase}} \in \{0,1\}$; the same networks then approximate the flow at any query point $(\mathbf{x}, t_{\text{phase}})$.

\subsubsection{Non-Newtonian Molecular Viscosity (Carreau--Yasuda)}
\label{sec:carreau}

Blood exhibits shear-thinning: viscosity decreases with increasing shear rate. We use the Carreau--Yasuda model
\begin{equation}
\mu(\dot{\gamma}) = \mu_\infty + (\mu_0 - \mu_\infty) \left[ 1 + (\lambda \dot{\gamma})^a \right]^{(n-1)/a},
\label{eq:carreau}
\end{equation}
with $\mu_\infty = 0.0035$\,Pa\,s, $\mu_0 = 0.16$\,Pa\,s, $\lambda = 8.2$\,s, $n = 0.2128$, $a = 0.64$. The shear rate is $\dot{\gamma} = \sqrt{2\,\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{D}^2)}$ where $\mathbf{D} = \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\mathbf{u} + (\nabla\mathbf{u})^T)$. In the momentum residual we use $\mu(\dot{\gamma})$ at each collocation point; to avoid prohibitively expensive third derivatives, we adopt a \emph{quasi-steady} approximation: $\mu_{\text{mol}}$ is treated as a spatially varying coefficient when forming $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{eff}}$, and only $\nabla\nu_t$ is differentiated through the computational graph, consistent with lagged viscosity updates in conventional RANS solvers.

\subsubsection{Boundary Conditions}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Wall} $\Gamma_{\text{wall}}$: no-slip $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}$. Pressure and WSS are supervised from CFD on the same wall points.
\item \textbf{Inlet} $\Gamma_{\text{in}}$: uniform axial velocity with magnitude $V_{\text{inlet}} = 0.5$\,m/s (systole) and $0.1$\,m/s (diastole); transverse components are set to zero.
\item \textbf{Outlet} $\Gamma_{\text{out}}$: zero gauge pressure $p = 0$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Wall Shear Stress}

WSS is the tangential component of the traction $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{eff}}\,\mathbf{n}$ at the wall. With inward unit normal $\mathbf{n}$,
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\tau}_w = \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{eff}}\,\mathbf{n} - (\mathbf{n}^T \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{eff}}\,\mathbf{n})\,\mathbf{n}, \qquad \text{WSS} = |\boldsymbol{\tau}_w| \;\text{ or }\; \boldsymbol{\tau}_w \text{ (vector)}.
\label{eq:wss}
\end{equation}
At the wall, $\mu_{\text{eff}}$ uses the same Carreau--Yasuda $\mu(\dot{\gamma})$ computed from the velocity gradients together with the learned $\nu_t$; WSS is then obtained from the velocity derivatives via automatic differentiation and compared to CFD WSS in the loss. Crucially, WSS is not a direct network output but a derived quantity, meaning the velocity networks must learn gradients that are consistent with the observed surface tractions.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Non-Dimensionalisation and Standardisation}
\label{sec:nondim}
%==============================================================================

To keep activations and loss scales of order unity, we non-dimensionalise and then standardise inputs and targets.

\subsubsection{Reference Scales}

From the CFD data we set $P_{\text{ref}} = \max|\text{pressure}|$, then
\begin{equation}
U_{\text{ref}} = \sqrt{P_{\text{ref}}/\rho}, \quad \tau_{\text{ref}} = \mu_\infty U_{\text{ref}}/L_{\text{ref}}, \quad \mathrm{Re} = \frac{\rho U_{\text{ref}} L_{\text{ref}}}{\mu_\infty}.
\end{equation}
Spatial coordinates are centred with the domain mean and scaled: $\mathbf{x}_{\text{bar}} = (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_{\text{mean}})/L_{\text{ref}}$, then $\mathbf{x}_{\text{std}} = \mathbf{x}_{\text{bar}} / c_s$ so that $\mathbf{x}_{\text{std}}$ lies in $[-1,1]^3$ (with $c_s$ the coordinate scale). Velocities and pressure are non-dimensionalised as $\mathbf{u}_{\text{bar}} = \mathbf{u}/U_{\text{ref}}$, $p_{\text{bar}} = p/P_{\text{ref}}$. Turbulent viscosity is non-dimensionalised as $\nu_{t,\text{bar}} = \nu_t/(U_{\text{ref}} L_{\text{ref}})$.

\subsubsection{Standardisation of Targets}

Pressure and WSS targets are standardised so that data losses are $\mathcal{O}(1)$: $p_{\text{std}} = p_{\text{bar}}/\sigma_p$, $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{w,\text{std}} = \boldsymbol{\tau}_w/\tau_{\text{ref}}/\sigma_{\tau}$, where $\sigma_p$ and $\sigma_\tau$ are the standard deviations of the non-dimensional pressure and WSS over the full dataset. The network outputs $p$ in standardised form; for WSS we predict the three components in the same standardised space and compute the loss against CFD WSS converted to that space. The physics residual is expressed in non-dimensional form (using $\mathbf{u}_{\text{bar}}$, $p_{\text{bar}}$, $\nu_{t,\text{bar}}$, and $\mu/\mu_\infty$ from Carreau--Yasuda) so that the momentum and continuity residuals are dimensionally consistent.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Network Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}
%==============================================================================

We use \emph{five} separate scalar-valued networks sharing the same input $(x,y,z,t_{\text{phase}})$: four for the flow variables ($u$, $v$, $w$, $p$) and one for the non-dimensional turbulent viscosity $\nu_{t,\text{bar}}$. Each flow branch has the same topology with hidden dimension $H=128$ and $L=6$ residual blocks; the $\nu_t$ branch is deliberately smaller ($H=64$, $L=4$) because the turbulent viscosity field is spatially smoother and benefits from a more regularised representation. Figure~\ref{fig:arch_overview} gives a high-level view; Figure~\ref{fig:arch_branch} details a single branch.

\subsubsection{Input and Output}

The input is the 4-vector $\mathbf{z} = (x_{\text{std}}, y_{\text{std}}, z_{\text{std}}, t_{\text{phase}})$. Each of the five networks outputs a single scalar: $\mathrm{Net}_u(\mathbf{z}) \to u_{\text{bar}}$, $\mathrm{Net}_v \to v_{\text{bar}}$, $\mathrm{Net}_w \to w_{\text{bar}}$, $\mathrm{Net}_p \to p_{\text{std}}$, $\mathrm{Net}_{\nu_t} \to \nu_{t,\text{bar}}$. The $\nu_t$ output must remain strictly positive, which we enforce through the transformation $\nu_{t,\text{bar}} = \mathrm{softplus}(\mathrm{raw} + \zeta) + \nu_{t,\min}$, where $\nu_{t,\min} = 0.001$ provides a hard floor and $\zeta = 2.0$ is a fixed shift that places the initial softplus operating point at $\mathrm{sigmoid}(2) \approx 0.88$, well away from the vanishing-gradient regime near zero. The decoder bias is initialised so that $\mathrm{softplus}(b + \zeta) \approx 0.05$, giving a physically reasonable starting turbulent viscosity across the domain.

\subsubsection{Fourier Feature Encoding}

Following Tancik et al.\ \cite{ref_tancik2020}, we pass $\mathbf{z}$ through a Fourier feature layer before the main MLP. With a fixed random matrix $\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times K}$ ($K=16$ frequencies, entries drawn i.i.d.\ from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ with scale $\sigma = 1.0$),
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{z}) = \bigl[ \sin(2\pi \mathbf{B}^T \mathbf{z}), \; \cos(2\pi \mathbf{B}^T \mathbf{z}) \bigr] \in \mathbb{R}^{2K}.
\end{equation}
This expands the input to $2K = 32$ dimensions and improves the network's ability to represent higher-frequency spatial structure, which is important for resolving shear layers and pressure gradients. The random matrix $\mathbf{B}$ is drawn once at initialisation and held fixed throughout training.

\subsubsection{Residual MLP (Per Branch)}

Each branch maps $\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{z})$ through a linear encoder to hidden dimension $H$, followed by Swish activation, then a stack of residual blocks, and finally a linear decoder to the scalar output. Each residual block takes the form
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}^{(\ell+1)} = \mathbf{h}^{(\ell)} + \mathbf{W}_2^{(\ell)}\, \sigma\bigl( \mathbf{W}_1^{(\ell)} \mathbf{h}^{(\ell)} \bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ denotes the Swish activation ($\sigma(x) = x \cdot \mathrm{sigmoid}(x)$). Notably, no activation is applied after the skip addition, preserving the linear pathway for gradient flow. All weights are initialised with Kaiming normal, and biases are initialised to zero. This architecture yields smooth, infinitely differentiable outputs---a requirement for computing PDE residuals and WSS via automatic differentiation. For the flow branches, the pipeline is: 4D input $\to$ 32D Fourier features $\to$ 128D encoder $\to$ 6 residual blocks (128D) $\to$ 1D decoder. The $\nu_t$ branch follows the same pattern at reduced scale (64D, 4 blocks) with the softplus output transform.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Loss Function}
\label{sec:loss}
%==============================================================================

The total loss is a weighted sum of data-fitting and physics-enforcement terms. Rather than relying on manually tuned fixed weights, we employ a gradient-norm adaptive weighting scheme that dynamically rebalances the loss components throughout training based on their gradient magnitudes. Initial weights provide a starting configuration, and the adaptive mechanism adjusts them every 100 epochs to prevent any single term from dominating gradient updates.

\subsubsection{Data and Boundary Terms}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{WSS loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\text{WSS}}$: mean squared error between predicted and CFD WSS (all three components) on wall points, computed in standardised space. The predicted WSS is obtained by differentiating the velocity networks and forming the tangential traction (Eq.~\ref{eq:wss}), incorporating the Carreau-Yasuda viscosity ratio. To manage GPU memory, wall points are processed in batches of 16{,}000.
\item \textbf{Pressure loss} $\mathcal{L}_p$: MSE between predicted and CFD pressure on wall points (standardised).
\item \textbf{No-slip loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\text{wall}}$: MSE of $u,v,w$ on wall points (target: zero).
\item \textbf{Inlet loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\text{inlet}}$: MSE between predicted velocity and the prescribed axial $V_{\text{inlet}}$ on inlet cross-section points, with transverse components penalised toward zero.
\item \textbf{Outlet loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\text{outlet}}$: MSE of pressure on outlet cross-section points (target: zero gauge).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Physics Loss}

At a set of \emph{collocation points} $\{\mathbf{x}_i\}_{i=1}^{N_c}$ sampled in the interior of $\Omega$, we form the continuity residual $r_c = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}$ and the three momentum residuals $r_{m,x}, r_{m,y}, r_{m,z}$ from the left-hand side minus the right-hand side of the non-dimensional RANS momentum equation (including $\mu_{\text{eff}} = \mu_{\text{mol}}(\dot{\gamma}) + \nu_{t,\text{bar}}$ and the quasi-steady treatment of $\nabla\mu_{\text{mol}}$). The physics loss is then
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}} = \frac{1}{N_c} \sum_{i=1}^{N_c} \bigl( r_c^2 + r_{m,x}^2 + r_{m,y}^2 + r_{m,z}^2 \bigr).
\end{equation}
All spatial derivatives of $\mathbf{u}$, $p$, and $\nu_t$ with respect to $\mathbf{x}_{\text{std}}$ are obtained via automatic differentiation; the chain rule converts them to the non-dimensional physical space used in the PDE. We use $N_c = 6{,}000$ interior collocation points per cardiac phase, processed in minibatches of 3{,}000 to control memory usage.

\subsubsection{$\nu_t$ Lower-Bound Regularisation}

Without explicit regularisation, the $\nu_t$ network can collapse to near-zero values---a trivial solution that simplifies the momentum residual at the expense of physical fidelity. To prevent this, we impose a soft lower-bound penalty at each collocation point:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\nu_t} = \frac{\lambda_{\nu_t}}{N_c} \sum_{i=1}^{N_c} \max\bigl(0, \;\nu_{t,\text{target}} - \nu_{t,\text{bar}}(\mathbf{x}_i)\bigr)^2,
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\nu_t} = 100$ and $\nu_{t,\text{target}} = 0.05$. This point-wise formulation ensures that each collocation point provides an independent gradient signal to the $\nu_t$ network, preventing batch averaging from washing out spatial information. Combined with the hard floor ($\nu_{t,\min} = 0.001$) in the network output layer, this dual constraint keeps the turbulent viscosity in a physically meaningful range.

\subsubsection{Total Loss}

The total loss combines all terms with learnable adaptive weights:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \lambda_{\text{WSS}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{WSS}} + \lambda_p\,\mathcal{L}_p + \lambda_{\text{wall}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{wall}} + \lambda_{\text{inlet}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{inlet}} + \lambda_{\text{outlet}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{outlet}} + \lambda_{\text{phys}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}} + \mathcal{L}_{\nu_t}.
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
The initial (seed) weights are $\lambda_{\text{WSS}} = 1$, $\lambda_p = 10$, $\lambda_{\text{wall}} = \lambda_{\text{inlet}} = \lambda_{\text{outlet}} = 10$, and $\lambda_{\text{phys}} = 0.01$. Boundary conditions receive stronger initial weighting because their satisfaction provides the geometric scaffolding on which the interior physics solution is built: without accurate no-slip, inlet, and outlet conditions, the RANS residual loses its physical grounding. The physics weight starts low to allow the data-fitting terms to establish a reasonable velocity and pressure field before the PDE constraints begin shaping the interior solution. These initial weights are then continuously adjusted by the adaptive scheme described next.

\subsubsection{Gradient-Norm Adaptive Loss Weighting}
\label{sec:adaptive_weights}

Training a PINN involves minimising a composite objective whose individual terms can have gradient magnitudes that differ by orders of magnitude. If left unbalanced, terms with large gradients dominate the parameter updates while terms with small gradients are effectively ignored, leading to poor convergence or physically inconsistent solutions. We address this with a gradient-norm balancing strategy inspired by Wang et al.\ \cite{ref_wang2021}, adapted to our multi-loss setting.

Every $T_{\text{update}} = 100$ epochs, we compute the mean absolute gradient of each loss component with respect to all flow network parameters:
\begin{equation}
G_i = \frac{1}{|\boldsymbol{\theta}|} \sum_{j} \left| \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_i}{\partial \theta_j} \right|, \qquad i \in \{\text{WSS}, p, \text{wall}, \text{inlet}, \text{outlet}, \text{phys}\}.
\end{equation}
We designate the WSS loss as the reference (since WSS prediction is the primary clinical output) and compute the raw rebalancing weight for each term as $\hat{\lambda}_i = G_{\text{WSS}} / G_i$. To prevent oscillatory weight updates, we smooth with an exponential moving average:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_i^{(t)} = (1 - \alpha)\,\lambda_i^{(t-1)} + \alpha\,\hat{\lambda}_i, \qquad \alpha = 0.9.
\end{equation}
Two safeguards prevent degenerate weighting: a floor on the physics weight ($\lambda_{\text{phys}} \geq 0.1$) ensures that the governing equations always contribute meaningfully, and a global cap ($\lambda_i \leq 20$) prevents any term whose loss has nearly vanished from receiving disproportionately large weight. The full procedure is summarised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive_weights}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Gradient-Norm Adaptive Loss Weighting}
\label{alg:adaptive_weights}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Loss terms $\{\mathcal{L}_i\}$, network parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, current weights $\{\lambda_i\}$
\Require Smoothing $\alpha = 0.9$, floor $\lambda_{\text{phys}}^{\min} = 0.1$, cap $\lambda^{\max} = 20$
\For{each loss $\mathcal{L}_i$}
    \State $G_i \gets \text{mean}(|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}_i|)$ \Comment{mean absolute gradient norm}
\EndFor
\State $G_{\text{ref}} \gets G_{\text{WSS}}$ \Comment{reference loss}
\For{each loss $\mathcal{L}_i$}
    \State $\hat{\lambda}_i \gets G_{\text{ref}} / G_i$
    \State $\lambda_i \gets (1-\alpha)\,\lambda_i + \alpha\,\hat{\lambda}_i$ \Comment{EMA smoothing}
    \State $\lambda_i \gets \min(\lambda_i, \lambda^{\max})$ \Comment{cap}
\EndFor
\State $\lambda_{\text{phys}} \gets \max(\lambda_{\text{phys}}, \lambda_{\text{phys}}^{\min})$ \Comment{physics floor}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training}
%==============================================================================

\subsubsection{Alternating Dual-Optimiser Strategy}

A central design choice in our training procedure is the use of two separate AdamW optimisers with an alternating update scheme. The \emph{flow optimiser} controls the parameters of the four flow networks ($u$, $v$, $w$, $p$) and is updated using the full composite loss $\mathcal{L}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:total_loss}), so that velocity and pressure are shaped by data fitting, boundary conditions, and physics enforcement simultaneously. The \emph{turbulent viscosity optimiser} controls Net$_{\nu_t}$ and is updated using \emph{only} $\mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}} + \mathcal{L}_{\nu_t}$---the PDE residual and the soft lower-bound penalty. This separation is motivated by the observation that data-driven gradients (WSS, pressure matching) provide no direct information about what the turbulent viscosity should be; routing these gradients to Net$_{\nu_t}$ would introduce noise and slow convergence. By receiving gradient signal exclusively from the governing equations, Net$_{\nu_t}$ learns the spatially-varying eddy viscosity field that makes the RANS momentum residual as small as possible---effectively performing an implicit turbulence closure.

Within each training epoch, the procedure iterates over both cardiac phases and for each phase:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute the full loss $\mathcal{L}$ and backpropagate through the flow network parameters. Apply gradient clipping (max norm 1.0) and step the flow optimiser.
\item Separately compute $\mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}} + \mathcal{L}_{\nu_t}$ and backpropagate through the $\nu_t$ network parameters. Apply gradient clipping and step the $\nu_t$ optimiser.
\end{enumerate}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:training} gives the complete per-epoch training procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Per-Epoch Training Loop with Alternating Dual-Optimiser}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Networks $\boldsymbol{\theta} = \{\theta_u, \theta_v, \theta_w, \theta_p, \theta_{\nu_t}\}$, flow optimiser $\mathcal{O}_{\text{flow}}$, $\nu_t$ optimiser $\mathcal{O}_{\nu_t}$
\Require Loss weights $\{\lambda_i\}$, gradient clip $c = 1.0$, adaptive interval $T_{\text{update}} = 100$
\Require Max epochs $E_{\max} = 10{,}000$, early-stopping patience $P = 3{,}000$
\For{$e = 1, \ldots, E_{\max}$}
    \For{each cardiac phase $s \in \{\text{systolic},\,\text{diastolic}\}$}
        \State \textbf{// Step 1: update flow networks}
        \State Compute $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$ \hspace{0.5em}(Eq.~\ref{eq:total_loss}) using current $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
        \State $\mathcal{O}_{\text{flow}}$.zero\_grad(); \; $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$.backward()
        \State Clip gradients of $\{\theta_u,\theta_v,\theta_w,\theta_p\}$ to max norm $c$
        \State $\mathcal{O}_{\text{flow}}$.step()
        \State \textbf{// Step 2: update turbulent viscosity network}
        \State Compute $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_t\text{-update}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}} + \mathcal{L}_{\nu_t}$ using current $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
        \State $\mathcal{O}_{\nu_t}$.zero\_grad(); \; $\mathcal{L}_{\nu_t\text{-update}}$.backward()
        \State Clip gradients of $\theta_{\nu_t}$ to max norm $c$
        \State $\mathcal{O}_{\nu_t}$.step()
    \EndFor
    \If{$e \bmod T_{\text{update}} = 0$}
        \State Update $\{\lambda_i\}$ via Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive_weights} \Comment{gradient-norm rebalancing}
    \EndIf
    \State Decay learning rates via cosine annealing (Eq.~\ref{eq:cosine})
    \If{no improvement in $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$ by $> 10^{-6}$ for $P$ consecutive epochs}
        \State \textbf{break} \Comment{early stopping}
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Optimiser Hyperparameters}

The flow optimiser uses a base learning rate of $10^{-4}$ with AdamW ($\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.99$, $\varepsilon = 10^{-15}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$). The $\nu_t$ optimiser uses a $10\times$ higher learning rate ($10^{-3}$) with reduced weight decay ($10^{-5}$), reflecting the need for the smaller $\nu_t$ network to adapt quickly to the evolving flow field. Both learning rates are decayed via a cosine annealing schedule from their initial values to $\eta_{\min} = 10^{-6}$ over the full 10{,}000 training epochs:
\begin{equation}
\eta(e) = \eta_{\min} + \frac{1}{2}(\eta_0 - \eta_{\min})\left(1 + \cos\frac{\pi e}{E_{\max}}\right).
\label{eq:cosine}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Wall Data and Interior Collocation}

Wall data (coordinates, pressure, WSS) are subsampled from the full CFD wall mesh by a factor of three, retaining approximately one third of the available surface points whilst maintaining spatial coverage across the aneurysm geometry. Wall normals are estimated from the subsampled point cloud using Open3D's local PCA-based algorithm with a hybrid KD-tree search (search radius 0.01\,m, maximum 30 nearest neighbours). The resulting normals are oriented consistently inward by checking the dot product of each normal with the vector from the surface point to the domain centroid; normals pointing outward are flipped. These inward normals serve a dual purpose: they define the surface orientation needed for WSS computation (Eq.~\ref{eq:wss}), and they provide the directions along which interior collocation points are generated.

Interior collocation points, where the physics loss is evaluated, are not sampled uniformly at random throughout the lumen. Instead, they are generated by displacing wall surface points inward along their estimated normals by a random offset drawn uniformly from $[0.05, 0.5]$ in normalised coordinates. This near-wall concentration is deliberate: the steepest velocity gradients---and hence the largest RANS residuals---occur in the boundary layer adjacent to the vessel wall, precisely where physics enforcement is most critical. We sample $N_c = 6{,}000$ collocation points per cardiac phase. These points are generated once at the start of training and held fixed throughout, as we found that periodic resampling offered no improvement with the wall-normal sampling strategy.

\subsubsection{Early Stopping and Checkpointing}

Training runs for a maximum of 10{,}000 epochs. The model with the lowest total training loss is checkpointed. Early stopping is enabled with a patience of 3{,}000 epochs and a minimum improvement threshold of $10^{-6}$; if the loss does not improve by at least this amount over 3{,}000 consecutive epochs, training terminates. Since PINNs do not use a separate validation split---the physics loss inherently regularises the solution---we monitor the training loss directly.

\subsection{CFD Ground Truth Data}

Reference CFD simulations providing the ground truth data for PINN training were performed using ANSYS Fluent. The simulations employed the SST $k$--$\omega$ Transition Model to capture turbulent flow characteristics, coupled with the Carreau-Yasuda non-Newtonian viscosity model to represent blood rheology. Temporal discretisation used a time step of 0.001~s, confirmed through independence studies to ensure temporal convergence. Mesh independence was verified through systematic refinement, ultimately selecting element sizes between 0.7--0.9~mm that yielded WSS variations below 2\% when compared to finer meshes. The pressure-based finite volume solver advanced the solution with a residual error threshold of $10^{-3}$ at each time step. Simulations ran for 2 seconds of physical time, encompassing 2000 time steps and capturing four complete cardiac cycles to ensure fully developed periodic flow.

\subsection{Dataset Composition}

The dataset for each configuration comprises wall boundary data distributed over the vessel and aneurysm surfaces, containing spatial coordinates $(x, y, z)$ along with pressure $p$ and wall shear stress (WSS) values at each location. The total number of wall points across all configurations is approximately 801{,}586, of which approximately one third (after subsampling by a factor of three) is used for training. By the no-slip condition, velocity at these wall points equals zero. The wall-focused dataset thus provides the pressure and WSS distributions that, combined with the physics constraints enforced at interior collocation points, enable reconstruction of the full three-dimensional flow field---including the velocity in the interior where no direct measurements are supplied to the network.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We assess model performance through a combination of quantitative error metrics and qualitative visualisations. Quantitatively, we compute mean absolute error (MAE), root mean squared error (RMSE), the coefficient of determination ($R^2$), and relative error percentages for each predicted variable.

For qualitative assessment, we generate spatial visualisations including three-panel comparisons---showing CFD ground truth, PINN prediction, and absolute error---for velocity magnitude, pressure, and wall shear stress across XY and XZ planes during both cardiac phases. Centreline profiles extracted along the primary flow axis enable detailed examination of streamwise variations. Phase-specific comparisons evaluate the network's ability to capture temporal variations between peak systolic and minimum diastolic flow conditions.

\subsection{Phase-Specific Analysis}

The systolic phase, characterised by higher velocities approaching 0.5~m/s and peak WSS values around 3~Pa, presents a more challenging prediction scenario due to complex flow patterns featuring stronger recirculation zones. Despite this complexity, the PINN achieves coefficients of determination between 0.88 and 0.91 for velocity predictions during systole. The diastolic phase exhibits considerably lower velocities (approximately 0.1~m/s) and reduced WSS values (roughly 0.5~Pa), accompanied by simpler flow structures. Interestingly, the PINN achieves slightly lower $R^2$ values (0.85--0.89) for velocity during diastole, likely attributable to reduced signal-to-noise ratios when flow magnitudes are small. The discrete phase encoding ($t_{\text{phase}} \in \{0, 1\}$) successfully drives these temporal variations throughout the domain.

\subsection{Computational Efficiency}

Training a PINN for a single aneurysm configuration requires approximately 2 hours on a standard NVIDIA GPU, with early stopping typically activating before the full 10{,}000 epochs. Across all six configurations, total training amounts to roughly 12 hours. Once trained, evaluation across the full dataset completes in under 10 seconds, whilst querying a single spatial location requires less than 1 millisecond.

When compared to the reference CFD simulations, which demanded 8--12 hours per configuration, the PINN provides near-instantaneous inference after the initial training investment. This distinction becomes pronounced in scenarios requiring repeated queries, such as parametric studies or clinical decision support, where the PINN provides speedups exceeding three orders of magnitude.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Challenge of Learning RANS from Sparse Data}

Inferring volumetric turbulent viscosity ($\nu_t$) using only surface measurements (wall WSS and pressure) presents a significant challenge for PINNs. In traditional CFD, $\nu_t$ is resolved using complex $k$--$\omega$ transport equations integrated over the entire domain. In our framework, the PINN must learn this turbulent field implicitly through the momentum residuals. Standard joint optimisation fails in this scenario, as the network invariably collapses the turbulent viscosity to zero (reverting to a laminar flow regime) to more easily satisfy the boundary data losses. The model successfully learns the RANS physics only through the introduction of the alternating optimiser strategy, combined with the lower-bound regularisation and adaptive weight bounding.

\subsection{Importance of Explicit Boundary Conditions}

Beyond standard no-slip walls, providing explicit, time-dependent inlet velocity profiles and zero-pressure outlet conditions proved essential to constrain the internal flow field. Even without interior velocity supervision, explicitly enforcing the velocity magnitude at the inlet and pressure at the outlet allows the PINN to properly resolve complex internal haemodynamics. Without these bounding constraints, the network struggles to accurately capture critical internal features, such as the recirculation zones within the aneurysm sac.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Learning Effectiveness}

Our results provide evidence that embedding physical laws directly into neural network training offers substantial advantages over purely data-driven approaches. By incorporating RANS residuals evaluated at collocation points throughout the domain, the PINN achieves coefficients of determination exceeding 0.85 for velocity components whilst training on approximately one third of the available CFD wall data. Traditional machine learning models lacking physics constraints typically require substantially more data to reach comparable accuracy levels \cite{ref15}, highlighting the benefit of combining sparse measurements with conservation laws.

Beyond efficiency, physics-informed learning guarantees physical consistency that pure interpolation cannot ensure. The explicit enforcement of the continuity equation prevents non-physical artefacts such as spontaneous mass generation or sink regions that might otherwise emerge in data-sparse areas. The strong initial weighting applied to boundary losses ($\lambda_{\text{wall}} = \lambda_{\text{inlet}} = \lambda_{\text{outlet}} = 10$) ensures accurate satisfaction of the no-slip and inlet/outlet conditions, both critical for maintaining haemodynamic realism throughout the domain.

\subsection{Adaptive Weighting Strategy}

The gradient-norm adaptive weighting scheme (\S\ref{sec:adaptive_weights}) proved essential for training stability and convergence quality. In preliminary experiments with fixed weights, we observed that the physics loss gradient magnitude exceeded that of the WSS loss by up to two orders of magnitude during early training, causing the optimiser to prioritise PDE satisfaction at the expense of data fidelity. Conversely, when the physics weight was set too low, the network produced smooth but physically inconsistent velocity fields that violated mass conservation.

The adaptive mechanism resolves this tension by continuously monitoring gradient magnitudes and rebalancing weights so that all loss components contribute comparably to parameter updates. The choice of WSS as the reference loss anchors the weighting to the primary clinical quantity of interest, ensuring it always receives meaningful gradient signal. The EMA smoothing ($\alpha = 0.9$) prevents rapid oscillations in the weights while still allowing the scheme to track the shifting loss landscape as training progresses. The physics weight floor ($\lambda_{\text{phys}} \geq 0.1$) provides an important safeguard: without it, the adaptive mechanism can inadvertently suppress physics enforcement once data losses become dominant, leading to solutions that fit the wall data but violate the governing equations in the interior.

We found this scheme superior to a fixed linear ramp of the physics weight (tested in earlier iterations), as the adaptive mechanism naturally modulates physics enforcement in response to the actual training state rather than following a predetermined schedule.

\subsection{Non-Newtonian Viscosity Impact}

Incorporating the Carreau-Yasuda model significantly improves WSS predictions compared to constant-viscosity Newtonian models. In low-shear aneurysm sacs, the shear-thinning effect reduces effective viscosity by up to 30\%, directly impacting WSS magnitudes. This is particularly relevant for rupture risk assessment, as WSS thresholds for wall degradation are viscosity-dependent \cite{ref18}.

\subsection{Multi-Configuration Generalisation}

Consistent performance across six geometries (Table~\ref{tab:performance}, low standard deviation) indicates that the PINN methodology generalises across diverse aneurysm morphologies rather than overfitting to specific configurations. However, training separate models per geometry was necessary; a single universal model trained on all configurations exhibited degraded performance, suggesting geometry-specific features dominate the haemodynamic response.

Future work could explore transfer learning or meta-learning approaches to enable knowledge sharing across geometries while retaining configuration-specific adaptation.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

Accurate WSS prediction is crucial for aneurysm rupture risk stratification. Our PINN achieves MAE $\approx$ 0.18~Pa and RMSE $\approx$ 0.31~Pa for WSS, well within the uncertainty range of CFD models themselves (typically $\pm$15--20\% due to boundary condition sensitivity \cite{ref19}).

The near-instantaneous inference enables interactive exploration of haemodynamic fields, allowing clinicians to query WSS values or velocity profiles at any location within seconds. The reduced computational requirements---running on standard GPU workstations rather than high-performance clusters---lower the barrier to entry for smaller clinical centres. Ensemble predictions from networks with different random initialisations can provide confidence intervals for uncertainty quantification.

Nevertheless, the approach remains dependent on high-quality CFD training data. Current implementations require geometry-specific models, and rigorous validation against in vivo measurements such as 4D flow MRI remains essential before clinical adoption can be justified.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Several limitations warrant acknowledgement. Our phase-specific approach predicts systolic and diastolic snapshots rather than capturing the full transient dynamics throughout the cardiac cycle; extending to continuous time-resolved simulation would provide richer temporal characterisation. Our PINN currently treats walls as rigid boundaries; incorporating compliant wall mechanics would enhance physiological realism.

Regarding turbulence modelling, whereas the reference CFD employs the full SST $k$--$\omega$ transition model with transport equations for turbulent kinetic energy $k$, specific dissipation rate $\omega$, intermittency $\gamma$, and momentum thickness Reynolds number $\text{Re}_\theta$, our PINN replaces this with a single learnable scalar field $\nu_t(\mathbf{x}; t_{\text{phase}})$ that must implicitly capture the combined effect of all these quantities. While this simplification is effective for the geometries studied here---where the eddy viscosity hypothesis provides a reasonable closure---it may lose accuracy in transitional flow regimes or in geometries where anisotropic Reynolds stresses play a significant role. Incorporating explicit transport equations for $k$ and $\omega$ into the PINN loss, thereby learning a physics-informed turbulence model rather than a purely data-driven one, represents a promising direction for future work.

The deterministic network provides point estimates without quantifying uncertainty---a limitation that Bayesian PINN formulations \cite{ref20} or ensemble methods could address. The wall-normal collocation strategy concentrates physics enforcement near the wall; residual-adaptive sampling \cite{ref21} that places additional points in high-error interior regions could further improve accuracy.

Promising research directions include continuous temporal modelling throughout the cardiac cycle, parametric models that encode geometry as input features for cross-patient generalisation, multi-fidelity learning combining sparse high-fidelity CFD with abundant low-fidelity simulations, and prospective clinical validation against 4D flow MRI measurements.

\section{Conclusion}

This study has presented a physics-informed neural network framework for predicting three-dimensional haemodynamics within thoracic aortic aneurysms. By combining approximately one third of available CFD wall measurements with enforcement of the Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations, non-Newtonian Carreau-Yasuda rheology, and physiologically realistic boundary conditions, we have demonstrated that PINNs can achieve coefficients of determination exceeding 0.85 for velocity and 0.80 for pressure across six anatomically distinct aneurysm configurations.

Several methodological contributions underpin these results. The gradient-norm adaptive loss weighting scheme dynamically balances competing loss terms, eliminating the need for manual weight tuning and ensuring that both data fidelity and physics enforcement receive adequate gradient signal. The alternating dual-optimiser strategy provides the learnable turbulent viscosity network with dedicated gradient signal from the governing equations, enabling it to learn a spatially-varying eddy viscosity field without interference from data-fitting gradients. The wall-normal collocation sampling strategy concentrates physics enforcement in the near-wall boundary layer where it matters most. Together with Fourier feature encoding and the Carreau-Yasuda non-Newtonian viscosity model, these techniques yield a framework that is both data-efficient and physically consistent.

These findings suggest that physics-informed machine learning represents a viable pathway towards efficient and clinically deployable haemodynamic analysis. Realising this potential will require continued development of continuous temporal modelling, robust uncertainty quantification, and prospective clinical validation against in vivo measurements.
